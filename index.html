Rabies Prevention

Understanding rabies and how to protect yourself and your community

What is Rabies?

Rabies is a deadly disease that can affect the brain and nervous system. Early symptoms include fever and headache, and as the disease progresses, it can cause confusion, paralysis, and even death.

How Rabies is Spread

Rabies is mainly spread through the saliva of infected animals, often through bites. The animals most likely to carry rabies are dogs, bats, raccoons, and other wild animals.

How to Protect Yourself

Vaccination is the best way to protect yourself and your pets from rabies. Make sure your pets get their rabies shots on time, and avoid interacting with wild animals.

If You're Bitten

  1. Wash the wound with soap and water immediately
  2. Go to a doctor right away - don't wait for symptoms
  3. You may need a rabies shot - treatment must start quickly
  4. Report the incident to local health authorities
  5. Follow all medical advice for post-exposure treatment

Prevention is Key

Remember: Rabies is almost always fatal once symptoms appear, but it is completely preventable with proper vaccination and timely medical treatment after exposure. The key is to act quickly and seek medical attention immediately after any animal bite.

Animal Bite Awareness

Know how to respond to animal bites and prevent them from happening

Different Types of Animal Bites

Animal bites can come from pets like dogs and cats or wild animals like raccoons and bats. Some animals carry more risk, so it's important to know which ones to be cautious around.

Domestic Animals

Dogs and cats are common sources of bites. Even vaccinated pets can cause injury, so proper handling and training are essential.

Wild Animals

Bats, raccoons, and other wild animals pose higher rabies risks. Never attempt to handle wild animals, even if they appear friendly.

Stray Animals

Unvaccinated strays present significant risks. Contact animal control rather than approaching stray animals yourself.

🩹 First Aid for Animal Bites

  • Clean the bite with soap and water - Wash thoroughly for at least 15 minutes
  • Apply pressure to stop any bleeding - Use a clean cloth or bandage
  • Use an antiseptic and cover the bite with a clean bandage
  • See a doctor, especially if the animal that bit you was acting strange or is wild
  • Document everything - Record details about the animal and incident

📞 Reporting Animal Bites

If you get bitten, report it to local authorities or animal control. They may need to quarantine the animal to check for rabies, and they can help you with next steps.

🛡️ Preventing Animal Bites

Train pets to be well-behaved and always supervise kids around animals. Teach children to approach animals carefully, and avoid contact with wild animals whenever possible.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about rabies prevention and animal bite response

General Questions

What should I do if I'm bitten by an animal? +

Wash the wound right away with soap and water, apply pressure to stop bleeding, and get medical help as soon as possible. Don't wait for symptoms to appear - seek immediate medical attention.

How do I know if an animal has rabies? +

Rabid animals may act strange, like being unusually aggressive or acting disoriented. If you're unsure, seek help from a professional. Never approach an animal that is acting abnormally.

Rabies Vaccination

How effective are rabies vaccines? +

Rabies vaccines are very effective if given before or shortly after exposure to the virus. The vaccine is nearly 100% effective when administered properly and on schedule.

Do all animals need rabies vaccines? +

Yes! All dogs and cats should be vaccinated against rabies, and other pets may need it too depending on where you live. Check with your veterinarian about vaccination schedules.

Animal Bite Treatment

What should I do after a dog bite? +

Follow the same first-aid steps: clean the wound, stop the bleeding, and get medical care. Rabies shots might be necessary, especially if the dog's rabies status is unclear. Always report the incident to local authorities.
